CSAT Question

Evaluate limn→∞(1n+1+1n+2+⋯+12n)limn→∞(1n+1+1n+2+⋯+12n). Hint: Graph sketching may help.

Approach 1

Hints

  1. What does the graph of 1x1x look like?
  2. Maybe put the terms of the sum on your graph?
  3. Can you represent each term as a shape with an area equal to the terms value?
  4. Can you come up with 2 continuous functions as upper and lower bounds for the terms of the sum? (Think of a way to link to the rectangles you drew in previous hint.)
  5. For n=5, the graph should look like this:
    What are the areas underneath the two functions, and how do they relate to the sum?
  6. Try using the squeeze theorem.

Solution

Each number 1k1k is equal to the area of the rectangle extending to the right of the number, having height 1k1k and width 1. By sketching, notice there are two obvious continuous functions bounding these rectangles, one above, i.e. 1x−11x−1, and one below, i.e. 1x1x, whose integrals will also bound the initial sum under the limit.

Concretely,

∫2n+1n+1dxx<∑k=n+12n1k<∫2n+1n+1dxx−1.∫n+12n+1dxx<∑k=n+12n1k<∫n+12n+1dxx−1.

At n→∞n→∞, both integrals become ln2ln⁡2.

Approach 2

Hints

  1. Write expression in summation notation. Furthermore, could you restrict the starting index of both summations to k=1k=1?
  2. You should get f(n)=∑2nk=11k−∑nk=11kf(n)=∑k=12n1k−∑k=1n1k. How can you rewrite this sum using parity?
  3. What is the Taylor expansion of ln(x+1)?

Solution

Write f(n)=∑2nk=11k−∑nk=11kf(n)=∑k=12n1k−∑k=1n1k and consider the terms of these two sums. By pairing the even terms of the first sum with the terms of the second sum, each pair gives 12k−1k=−12k12k−1k=−12k. We are left with the odd terms and the negative even terms, which give:
f(n)=1−12+13−14+⋯+12n−1−12n.f(n)=1−12+13−14+⋯+12n−1−12n.

This is the alternating harmonic series, which is known to converge to ln2ln⁡2 when n→∞n→∞. Alternatively, one can also expand (Taylor) the function ln(1+x)ln⁡(1+x) and take x=1.x=1.

Approach 3

Hints

  1. How does the expression change when n changes ?
  2. If we write f(n)=1n+1+1n+2+⋯+12nf(n)=1n+1+1n+2+⋯+12n. What is f(n)−f(n−1)f(n)−f(n−1)?
  3. How can you use the previous hint to express f(n)f(n) as a sum of 2n2n terms?
  4. Does the Taylor expansion of ln(1+x)ln(1+x) look familiar?

solution

Let f(n)=1n+1+1n+2+⋯+12nf(n)=1n+1+1n+2+⋯+12n. We aim to express f(n) as a different sum. To achive this, we use the fact that f(n)=f(1)+(f(2)−f(1))+⋯+(f(n)−f(n−1))f(n)=f(1)+(f(2)−f(1))+⋯+(f(n)−f(n−1)). Notice that all terms except f(n)f(n) get canceled in this expression. Now consider

f(k)−f(k−1)=12k−1+12k−1k=12k−1−12k.f(k)−f(k−1)=12k−1+12k−1k=12k−1−12k.
Now we can put this result into our formula for f(n)f(n) and hence obtain
f(n)=∑k=1n12k−1−12k=1−12+13−14+⋯+12n−1−12n.f(n)=∑k=1n12k−1−12k=1−12+13−14+⋯+12n−1−12n.
 Now we can notice that these are the first 2n2n terms of the Taylor expansion of ln2ln⁡2. Hence, limn→∞f(n)=ln2.limn→∞f(n)=ln⁡2.
